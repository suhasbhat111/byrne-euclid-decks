\textbf{Book~I.\enspace Prop.\ XIII. Theor.}
\medskip\noindent\itshape
When a straight line (\drawUnitLine{ED}) standing upon another straight
line (\drawUnitLine{BC}) makes angles with it; they are either two right
angles or together equal to two right angles.
\bigskip\noindent\upshape
If \drawUnitLine{ED} be $\perp$ to \drawUnitLine{BC} then,\\
\drawAngle{ADB,EDA} and $\drawAngle{CDE}=\drawTwoRightAngles$~(def.~10),\\[6pt]
but if \drawUnitLine{ED} be not $\perp$ to \drawUnitLine{BC},\\
draw $\drawUnitLine{AD}\perp\drawUnitLine{BC}$~(pr.~I.11);\\[4pt]
$\drawAngle{ADB}+\drawAngle{CDE,EDA}=\drawTwoRightAngles$~(const.),\\
$\drawAngle{ADB}=\drawAngle{CDE,EDA}=\drawAngle{EDA}+\drawAngle{CDE}$\\[4pt]
$\therefore \drawAngle{ADB}+\drawAngle{CDE,EDA}=\drawAngle{ADB}+\drawAngle{EDA}+\drawAngle{CDE}$~(ax.~II)\\[4pt]
$=\drawAngle{ADB,EDA}+\drawAngle{CDE}=\drawTwoRightAngles$.
\begin{flushright}Q.\ E.\ D.\end{flushright}
