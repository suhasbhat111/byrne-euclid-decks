\textbf{Book~I.\enspace Prop.\ XIX. Theor.}
\medskip\noindent\itshape
If in any triangle \drawLine[bottom]{CA,BC,AB} one angle \drawAngle{A}
be greater than another \drawAngle{B} the side \drawUnitLine{BC} which is
opposite to the greater angle, is greater than the side \drawUnitLine{CA}
opposite the less.
\bigskip\noindent\upshape\begin{center}
If \drawUnitLine{BC} be not greater than \drawUnitLine{CA} then must\\
$\drawUnitLine{BC}=$ or $<\drawUnitLine{CA}$.\\[6pt]
If $\drawUnitLine{BC}=\drawUnitLine{CA}$ then\\
$\drawAngle{A}=\drawAngle{B}$~(pr.~I.5);\\
which is contrary to the hypothesis.\\[6pt]
\drawUnitLine{BC} is not less than \drawUnitLine{CA}; for if it were,\\
$\drawAngle{A}<\drawAngle{B}$~(pr.~I.18)\\
which is contrary to the hypothesis:\\[4pt]
$\therefore \drawUnitLine{BC}>\drawUnitLine{CA}$.
\end{center}
\begin{flushright}Q.\ E.\ D.\end{flushright}
