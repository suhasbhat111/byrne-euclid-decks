\textbf{Book~I.\enspace Prop.\ XXV. Theor.}
\medskip\noindent\itshape
If two triangles have two sides (\drawUnitLine{AB} and
\drawUnitLine{CA}) of the one respectively equal to two sides
(\drawUnitLine{DE} and \drawUnitLine{FD}) of the other, but
their bases unequal, the angle subtended by the greater base
(\drawUnitLine{BC}) of the one, must be greater than the angle
subtended by the less base (\drawUnitLine{EF}) of the other.
\bigskip\noindent\upshape\begin{center}
$\drawAngle{A}=\mbox{, }>\mbox{ or }<\drawAngle{D}$\\[6pt]
$\drawAngle{A}$ is not equal to $\drawAngle{D}$\\
for if $\drawAngle{A}=\drawAngle{D}$ then $\drawUnitLine{CB}=\drawUnitLine{FE}$~(pr.~I.4)\\
which is contrary to the hypothesis;\\[4pt]
$\drawAngle{A}$ is not less than $\drawAngle{D}$\\
for if $\drawAngle{A}<\drawAngle{D}$\\
then $\drawUnitLine{CB}<\drawUnitLine{FE}$~(pr.~I.24),\\
which is also contrary to the hypothesis:\\[4pt]
$\therefore \drawAngle{A}>\drawAngle{D}$.
\end{center}
\begin{flushright}Q.\ E.\ D.\end{flushright}
