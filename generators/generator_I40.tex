\textbf{Book~I.\enspace Prop.\ XL. Theor.}
\medskip\noindent\itshape
Equal triangles (\drawPolygon[bottom]{ACD} and
\drawPolygon[bottom]{BEF}) on equal bases, and on the same
side, are between the same parallels.
\bigskip\noindent\upshape
If \drawUnitLine{AB} which joins the vertices of triangles
be not $\parallel\drawUnitLine{CD,DE,EF}$,\\
draw $\drawUnitLine{AG}\parallel\drawUnitLine{CD,DE,EF}$~(pr.~I.31),\\
meeting \drawUnitLine{EH}.\\
Draw \drawUnitLine{FG}.\\[4pt]
$\because \drawUnitLine{AG}\parallel\drawUnitLine{CD,DE,EF}$~(const.)\\
$\drawPolygon{ACD}=\drawPolygon{BEF,GBF}$~(pr.~I.38)\\
but $\drawPolygon{ACD}=\drawPolygon{BEF}$~(hyp.)\\[4pt]
$\therefore \drawPolygon{BEF}=\drawPolygon{BEF,GBF}$,\\
a part equal to the whole, which is absurd.\\[4pt]
$\therefore \drawUnitLine{AG}\not\parallel\drawUnitLine{CD,DE,EF}$:
and in the same manner it can be demonstrated that no other
line except \drawUnitLine{AB} is $\parallel\drawUnitLine{CD,DE,EF}$:
$\therefore \drawUnitLine{AB}\parallel\drawUnitLine{CD,DE,EF}$.
\begin{flushright}Q.\ E.\ D.\end{flushright}
